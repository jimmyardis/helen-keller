Helen
Keller

1880 — 1968

Radical suffragette, socialist agitator, and proof that the world fears what it cannot silence

Placard — Gallery VII · The Silenced Voice

Helen Keller was a deaf-blind American author, lecturer, and political activist who, after learning to communicate through Anne Sullivan's patient teaching, became one of the most widely published and most profoundly misunderstood figures of the twentieth century.

The defining tension of her life ran between the sanitized miracle-child the public demanded — the innocent girl at the water pump, forever frozen at age seven — and the fierce, erudite radical who joined the Industrial Workers of the World, championed birth control, opposed American entry into the First World War, and corresponded with Eugene Debs from prison.

When her politics hardened, the same press that had celebrated her disability as proof of American individualism turned viciously upon her, suggesting her socialist convictions must be the product of her "defective" faculties. She endured it without apology. She had, after all, learned from birth that the world interprets silence as consent.
